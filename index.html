The Problem

Multi-Tiered Systems of Support (MTSS), also called Response to Intervention or RTI, is how schools catch students who are falling behind and give them targeted help before they fall further. At my school, that framework worked for reading and not for math.

Reading had a system because state mandates required reading screening: students were screened, a reading specialist reviewed the data, grouped them into evidence-based interventions, and monitored their response. Math had no such engine. Screening for math was unreliable. The state required Acadience benchmark reporting for mathematics, but it never became a dependable screening-to-intervention pipeline, so general-education teachers were left to run math interventions on their own, with limited time, support, and access to evidence-based resources.

The predictable result: interventions were inconsistently delivered, rarely documented, and often never progress-monitored.

Reading

A specialist reviewed the data, assigned students to evidence-based intervention groups, and tracked response. A structured, documented system.

Math

No comparable system. Teachers ran interventions on their own: inconsistently delivered, rarely documented, and often unmonitored.

Why it mattered

When a struggling student reached the evaluation stage, teams often lacked documentation that adequate Tier 2 supports had been tried, so students could be referred for special-education evaluation on incomplete evidence. Implemented with fidelity, MTSS does the opposite: research links it to roughly 15% fewer specific-learning-disability identifications and measurable achievement gains (Ainsworth et al., 2024).

The Research

Rather than assume what teachers needed, I asked them. An anonymous, mixed-methods survey went to certified staff: Likert-scale items to surface the patterns, open-ended questions to explain them. It was analyzed with descriptive statistics and inductive thematic coding.

18
Teachers responded: quantitative ratings and open-ended reflections from the people doing the work.
3 themes
The same barriers surfaced again and again, across grade levels.

The barriers teachers named:

Time & workload

Little time to document interventions or progress-monitor alongside Tier 1 instruction.

Unclear process

Confusion about documentation expectations, monitoring frequency, and which interventions to use.

Inconsistency

No shared procedures or decision rules, so practice varied from one grade level to the next.

What teachers said would help

Standardized documentation tools, easier access to intervention data, ready-made grade-aligned intervention resources, and clearer guidance on how MTSS is supposed to work. So that is what I built.

What I Built

A data-informed MTSS system built directly from those findings: a resources-and-support portal, a staged documentation workflow, a SMART-goal generator, and a tiered library of evidence-based interventions. It's organized so a classroom teacher can move from concern to documented support without guessing at the process.

The full system is bigger than any one screen: documentation, intervention selection, and data, in one place.

And every piece answered something the survey named:

Unclear process →

A staged workflow, so every teacher follows the same path from concern to decision.

No standard tools →

Standardized documentation for each stage, organized to cut redundant data entry.

Hard to choose interventions →

A SMART-goal generator paired with a tiered library of grade-aligned, evidence-based interventions.

Progress went unmonitored →

Built-in progress logs, so response to intervention is captured as it happens.

A record ready for the meeting

The system generates the completed documentation forms from the data teachers enter, and gives each teacher a view filtered to their own students. It also keeps a log of every ongoing and past intervention cycle, so when the MTSS team meets to review needs, the history is already in front of them (what was tried, for how long, and how the student responded) instead of scattered across memory and paper.

Key Design Decisions
The educator decides; the system structures

The tool drafts a SMART goal from a chosen evidence-based practice and the teacher’s inputs, organizes the documentation, and tracks progress. It never makes the instructional decision. The educator reviews and approves every goal. Human-in-the-loop by design.

Grounded in the evidence base

Interventions and guidance align with the Institute of Education Sciences, the What Works Clearinghouse, NCII, and the Utah MTSS Framework: public, evidence-based standards, not one person’s preferences.

Aligned to the eligibility standard

The documentation isn’t busywork. It mirrors what state and federal rules require as the Response-to-Intervention record in a specific-learning-disability determination: high-quality core instruction, universal screening and progress monitoring, tiered evidence-based interventions, and documented parent communication. What a teacher logs cycle to cycle becomes exactly the evidence an eligibility team needs later (USBE Special Education Rules; 34 CFR § 300.309).

Built to flex

Organized into defined stages with reduced redundancy, so the process adapts as policies and resources change rather than breaking when they do.

Why It Matters

The takeaway reaches past one school: strengthening MTSS is less about adding effort and more about giving teachers a process that’s clear, efficient, and shared.

Because the system was designed from teacher feedback and aligned to evidence-based standards, it targets the exact barriers that keep MTSS from working. It's also built to be refined over time rather than replaced. Done with fidelity, that’s the kind of process that keeps students in the right supports and out of unnecessary evaluations.

What’s Next
Beyond math

Extend the same structure to additional subjects and tiers on the model already in place.

Keep listening

Refine iteratively with ongoing staff feedback, the way it was built in the first place.

A data dashboard

Surface the intervention logs in a live view, so teams see each student’s history and response at a glance. That turns the record into measurable outcomes.
